Queensland budget 2021: Winners and losers

By Matt Eaton

Find out where the Queensland government is spending money in the budget and what it means for you.

Winner: Health

The government has allocated $2 billion over the next four years to a hospital building fund, in an effort to address shortfalls in hospital beds as the state's population continues to grow.

For the 2021-22 financial year, this will include:

  • $177 million towards a new 174-bed public hospital in Springfield, attached to the Mater Private Hospital Springfield
  • $103.5 million towards creating 130 new beds and refurbishment of Caboolture Hospital
  • $92.4 million to expand Ipswich Hospital, including new mental health facilities
  • $90 million for an extra 206 beds at Logan Hospital
  • $66.4 million for upgrades to Nambour Hospital, Atherton Hospital and the Thursday Island Hospital and Primary Health Care Centre

There is $480 million devoted to the government's ongoing COVID-19 response to cover fever clinics, contact tracing and testing, the vaccination program and quarantine.

Neutral: Social housing

The state government has allocated $1 billion for a Housing Investment Fund.

This will pay for about 3,600 new houses over the next four years.

Overall, the government is expecting to build 6,365 new public housing dwellings by December 2025.

Treasurer Cameron Dick called this "one of the single biggest investments any government has made into housing".

But as of September last year, there were 26,397 households waiting on Queensland's Public Housing Register.

The welfare sector has been calling for a $4 billion investment to address the current housing shortages.

Karen Walsh from social justice advocacy group Micah Projects said the government's public housing investment was "a relief".

"Because we are really in the grip of a social housing crisis like we haven't seen before," Ms Walsh said.

"At the moment, we're seeing rough sleeping go up, we're seeing overcrowding go up, we're seeing rents double for people who have had rents at a lower rate.

"This is a very big commitment and we're very grateful the government's put that investment in.

"People are really struggling with housing and domestic violence and many people are becoming homeless because of domestic violence, so it's critical that we get that crisis response right and then people can have a home that is stable and affordable."

Loser: Motorists

Revenue from fines and forfeitures is forecast to grow by a whopping 39.3 per cent in 2021-22.

This is partly driven by the introduction of cameras that detect drivers using mobile phones or not wearing seatbelts.

Registration charges will rise in line with the consumer price index, but the government has revised that CPI figure down from 1.8 per cent to 1.7 per cent.

Winner: Education

The government has committed $16.8 billion to education.

From an allocation of $1.4 billion for new schools, there is $913.7 million to fund 10 new state schools expected to open by 2024.

Four new schools will open in 2023:

  • Yarrabilba
  • Ripley
  • Augustine Heights/Redbank Plains
  • Palmview (secondary school)

Six more primary schools will be built to open in 2024:

  • Redland Bay
  • Bellbird Park
  • Logan Reserve, Crestmead or Park Ridge

New secondary schools by 2024 at:

  • Springfield
  • Collingwood Park or Redbank Plains
  • Logan Reserve or Park Ridge

Another $508.3 million has been allocated to build or refurbish buildings in existing state schools

There is also $202.9 million over four years to support universal access to kindergarten.

Loser: Property buyers and sellers

Queensland's booming property market is estimated to have boosted the government's transfer duty revenue by a whopping 21.4 per cent over the 2020-21 year.

This is projected to rise by 24.5 per cent in the 2021-22 year, owing to the volume of sales and what the budget papers describe as "a further acceleration in property price growth".

Basically, this means Queenslanders selling properties will be paying a lot more money into the state's coffers — the government estimates this will bring in $4.59 billion for 2021-22, and $3.69 billion for the current financial year.

Neutral: Tourism

Treasurer Cameron Dick has announced a $140 million commitment on helping small businesses innovate and access new markets.

There is $15 million specifically targeting the tourism sector for 2021-22, aimed at creating sustainable jobs and helping the struggling sector recover from the ongoing impact of the COVID-19 pandemic. 

Another $9.9 million will be spent on improving the 94-kilometre Wangetti Trail from Palm Cove to Port Douglas.

Queensland Tourism Industry Council CEO Daniel Gschwind welcomed the budget's focus on improving skills in the workforce and said he would lobby the government to ensure his sector received its share.

"Our industry with its small businesses must be part of the recovery, so that support is definitely welcome," he said.

"Overall, we welcome the budget. We will work with the government to make sure the enormous investment in skills and small business goes towards our industry as well."

Winner: Jobseekers

A $3.34 billion Queensland Jobs Fund will spend $320 million over four years on a Skilling Queenslanders For Work initiative aimed at providing work-based training for 15,000 people a year.

Up to $140 million is allocated to a Back To Work program over four years aimed at helping eligible businesses start hiring.

Queensland's screen industry will also receive $71 million to help attract film and television projects over the next four years.

Neutral: Roads

Road building is at the centre of the state government's $14.7 billion spend on infrastructure over the 2021-22 financial year.

It is a continuation of a $52 billion capital works commitment over four years, but spending will be $147 million lower than for 2020-21.

The Treasurer says 61 per cent of the next financial year's spending will occur outside of Greater Brisbane.

This will include:

  • $1.1 billion for the Rockhampton Ring Road
  • $1 billion for a 26-kilometre Cooroy to Curra Road upgrade
  • $662.5 million for widening between Caboolture-Bribie Island Road and Steve Irwin Way
  • $514.3 million to build bridges and approaches on the Haughton River Floodplain
  • $481 million for road widening in stage three of the Cairns Southern Access Corridor 

Winner: Environment

In a bid to improve water quality in the Great Barrier Reef, $270.1 million is being allocated over five years for a Queensland Reef Water Quality program.

Funds from a new $500 million Carbon Reduction Investment Fund will be used to support the existing Land Restoration Fund to support private investment and support financially sustainable carbon markets.

The government is also establishing a $2 billion Queensland Renewable Energy and Hydrogen Jobs Fund to help government-owned corporations increase ownership of renewable energy and hydrogen projects.
