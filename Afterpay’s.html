Afterpay’s banking move turbocharges valuation debate

By Clancy Yeates
June 14, 2021 — 3.51pm
, register or subscribe to save articles for later.
Advertisement

In the next few months Afterpay will unveil its foray into retail banking, initially offering Westpac-backed transaction accounts aimed at millennial and Generation Z female shoppers.

If another business were doing this it might be of passing interest. But with Afterpay, a $30 billion fintech juggernaut, almost every issue facing the company attracts intense market scrutiny, and stark differences in opinion.

To the bulls, the banking play known as Afterpay Money proves the buy now, pay later (BNPL) business is much more than a provider of short-term interest-free instalment loans.

Morgan Stanley last week predicted Afterpay Money could be put to all sorts of lucrative uses in years to come, including referring customers for mortgages, providing cash-back offers for retailers, and offering cryptocurrency or share trading.

The analysts estimate the banking move would almost double Afterpay’s Australian revenue by 2025, and they gave the stock a $145 price target — some 40 per cent higher than current levels.

Meanwhile, the bears are unconvinced by the banking push, and have plenty of other doubts about the market’s lofty expectations of Afterpay. While they accept the growing popularity of its BNPL products as an alternative to credit cards, they are more focused on Afterpay’s future challenges, which range from stiffer competition and a potential regulatory crackdown to the need for capital to fund expansion.

Alas, the last year has been a tough one for Afterpay sceptics. While the company’s shares have retreated off the highs hit last February, the stock is still up 12-fold from its lows of March last year, to $103.52 a share at Friday’s close.

Yet standout bear UBS is sticking to its guns, saying Afterpay is worth just over a third of that, or $37 a share. In a report last week, UBS analysts crunched the numbers on what is likely to be an ongoing debate as Afterpay seeks to become a mainstream payment method: its high cost to retailers.

UBS analyst Tom Beadle says that on average, Afterpay costs merchants 4 per cent of the purchase price, compared to average payment costs of less than 1 percent for debit cards.

He says this will make it hard for Afterpay to become a truly global player with up to $300 billion in annual in transaction volume, let alone a business on the scale of PayPal, which processes about US$1.2 trillion in payments at a cost to merchants of 1.2 per cent.

“It’s an example of a disruption of a fairly competitive industry with a less efficient solution,” Beadle says.

Beadle is also sceptical about how much revenue Afterpay Money can contribute, saying banks do not generally make much from offering transaction accounts. The real benefit from the platform may be to make customers more “sticky,” he suggests.

Others who share these concerns think Afterpay will be forced to slash merchant fees, especially as it fends off competition from rivals such as PayPal and the Commonwealth Bank, both of which will offer BNPL services at a much lower price than Afterpay.

The Reserve Bank of Australia has also looked closely at BNPL firms’ practice of banning merchants from passing on these higher costs through a surcharge - which makes Afterpay seem “free” to consumers. The RBA decided not to ban the practice last month, but said that could change in the future.

It is against these concerns that some professional money managers struggle with the market’s $30 billion valuation of Afterpay, which is yet to report a profit.

Atlas Fund Management chief investment officer Hugh Dive says while Afterpay is a “great company” he can’t make its valuation stack up when considering its “regulatory arbitrage” and the pressure on its fees. “It’s hard to make a case that that does not go down to what the credit cards charge,” Dive says of Afterpay’s merchant fee.

However, the bulls counter that Afterpay offers far more than payments. Instead they see it as a marketing platform that bowls up millions of customers to merchants — and therefore say it doesn’t make sense to compare it with the payment services offered by a bank.

They are also happy to overlook the lack of profits today because they believe the business can be global in scale and grabbing that dominant share now should be Afterpay’s main focus.

Australia, its home market, is seen as an important testing ground for what else the company can do to make money. The most obvious add-on is to sell a host of financial services, but some analysts are thinking about other ways the company might create value for shareholders.

Investment bank Jarden said in a report last Friday it thought the 27 million monthly leads Afterpay sends to retailers, through its shop directory and loyalty schemes, meant it was increasingly valuable as an advertising marketplace business. Indeed they say when its value as a “marketplace” is included their implied valuation on the company is $149 a share.

Judging whether these predictions will come true clearly involves more than a bit of tea leaf reading. But there is no doubt Afterpay’s ascension to the ranks of Australia’s top league of listed companies has made it harder for investors to ignore.

Despite its size, however, the wild share price ride of the last year and the sharply divergent market views suggest it is a riskier investment than the established dividend-paying companies that dominate the ASX50.
